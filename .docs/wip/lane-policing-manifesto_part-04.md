## **Polymaths in a specialist world**

Part of why lane‑policing hits me so hard is that I'm not built as a narrow specialist.

I'm a multi‑passionate, borderline polymath type. I learned digital arts, I taught myself everything else.  I care deeply about art, design, tools, engine tech, workflows, production, culture. Over the years I didn't just "touch" those areas. I built real competence in them. My value is not that I am the single best pure engineer or pure artist. It is that I see how a decision in one lane will quietly blow up three lanes away.

On paper, modern studios say they want people like that. They talk about T‑shaped folks, cross‑functional collaboration, "connecting the dots." They say they want people who can bridge art and engineering, who can translate across disciplines, who can see the whole system instead of just their slice.

In reality, a lot of organizations are still wired for specialists.

Career ladders, titles, headcount plans, even ego structures are built around single‑lane identities. You are an Engineer, an Artist, a Designer. Your authority comes from staying tightly aligned with that role. The more multi‑passionate and cross‑disciplinary you become, the harder it is for the system to place you.

That is where the tension shows up.

If you are a polymath in a specialist world, your default mode is cross‑lane. You think about systems.  You ask questions everywhere. You challenge assumptions across the whole board. You see patterns other people miss because you've lived in their constraints. You are the one saying, "This tool's decision will kneecap content production six months from now," or "This design pillar sounds great, but it will explode animation and VFX."

To someone who only understands specialists, that does not look like value. It looks like you are wandering out of your box.

Lane‑policing is one way to push polymaths back into shapes the system recognizes. It is a request, sometimes a command, to stop using the full range of what you know and care about, and to play the smaller, simpler character on your business card.

The irony is that the more complex and ambitious a project is, the more it needs multi‑passionate, cross‑disciplinary people. Large worlds, new IP, systemic content, big online games – these are not specialist‑only problems. They are exactly the kind of problems polymaths are built to solve.

If your studio says it wants innovation and big bets, but treats polymaths and multi‑passionate people as threats instead of assets, you do not have a talent problem. You have a lens problem.

## **Where this way of working still fits: small, sharp teams**

The interesting part is that everything I am talking about – polymaths, cross‑lane thinking, real psychological safety, brutal honesty in service of the work – is exactly what small teams need to survive.

My first game was a handful of us in a loft, figuring it out as we went. Nobody had a narrow lane. The “graphics person” might also be the tools person. We didn’t even have an “engineer” – we were hacking in HyperCard.  The level designer might also be scripting interactions.  The 3d artist creating a room might devise the ‘puzzle’.  Whoever cared the most about the schedule that week was effectively production. We were not doing that because it was trendy. We were doing it because there was no other way to ship; there was no education that could tell us how to do it “right.”

Today's AA and indie teams are not that different.

They are small groups with limited budgets and big ambitions. They win by moving faster than bureaucracy, by letting ideas come from anywhere, by trusting the people who can see across systems and call out problems early. They do not have room for fake safety, for leaders who only want good news, or for lane‑policing as a management tool.

The stuff that breaks big, rigid organizations – polymaths, multi‑passionate weirdos, cross‑disciplinary critique – is the stuff that makes small, sharp teams dangerous in the best way.

In a strange way, the industry blowing itself up and shrinking back down might be sending us back to where I started: a few people in a room, physical or virtual, trying to build something they actually believe in, with everyone trusted to see and say what is real.

That is the loop I want to close in this next epoch. Take everything I learned in big studios and pour it back into small teams that actually want people to bring their whole lane‑breaking selves to the work.

In the age of AI, everyone can be a polymath. Anyone can autodidact and wear multiple hats. Pandora is out of the box, molds are broken, lanes no longer exist...

## **Critical thinking: question your own pillars first**

Under all of this is something we barely talk about on teams: critical thinking.

A lot of people hear "critical thinking" and think "be great at poking holes in other people's arguments." What I mean is different: actively and honestly examining information and your own beliefs, using evidence and logic, with a real willingness to change your mind.

For me, real critical thinking starts with one rule:

*Aim your sharpest questions at the things you believe most strongly.*

It is easy to tear into ideas you already dislike. It is much harder to stress‑test your favorites: the engine you are sure is right, the workflow you "know" is optimal, the story you tell yourself about which disciplines matter most. If you never interrogate those, you are not doing critical thinking. You are doing self‑defense.

The pillars under that look like:

- Curiosity over certainty: lead with "what am I missing?" instead of "how do I defend this?"  
- Question your own strongest assumptions first, especially when you are the most senior person in the room.  
- Evidence over ego: let data, experience, and logic weigh more than your attachment to being right.  
- Humility: accept that you can be wrong even in your specialty, and that other disciplines may see things you do not.

Psychological safety and critical thinking meet here. You cannot have a culture that truly questions its own pillars if people get punished for pointing at cracks in the foundation. And you absolutely cannot have it if leaders never model "here is something I was sure about last year that I am not so sure about now."

Great teams do not just invite critique of other people's lanes. They create space – and obligation – to question the lane markers themselves, instead of enforcing them through lane‑policing when it feels uncomfortable.

## **late‑career in the age of AI (prospering from chaos)**

I am writing this at a weird moment.

The games industry is going through what feels like an extinction event. The landscape evokes The Walking Dead. Whole studios are gone. Veteran teams scattered. Layoff waves rolling through year after year. AI is racing forward, grabbing attention and money, while old pipelines get squeezed and questioned.

I am late‑career in that storm. I have been doing this for decades. I have seen booms and busts, console cycles, platform shifts, acquisition cycles, and phoenix movements. This one feels different. It is not just a downturn. It is a complete and total restructure.

I do not want to just survive it. I want to prosper from the chaos; I see this as the perfect moment to exploit and innovate.

Not by pretending nothing is changing. Not by clinging to old ladders. I want to use everything I have learned – art, design, tools, engineering, production, leadership, and the scars from lane‑policing cultures – and aim it at what comes next.

AI is not going away. Layoffs will not magically reverse. The industry is going to be leaner, weirder, and more experimental for a while. That environment actually favors the kind of person I have become: cross‑disciplinary, multi‑passionate, re-skillable, able to see systems end‑to‑end and reinvent myself when I have to.

Late‑career, for me, is not winding down. It is changing games.

That looks like:

- Treating AI as a force multiplier for small, sharp teams, not as a replacement for people who care.  
- Choosing work where polymaths are a feature, not a bug.  
- Refusing to trade truth and excellence for nice‑sounding internal PR.  
- Staying brutally curious about tools, models, pipelines, and people.  
- Using experience to build cultures where psychological safety and critical thinking are non‑negotiable.

I do not know exactly what the next epoch of games will look like. Nobody does. What I do know is the role I want to play in it: the person who walks into the chaos with eyes open, asks the hard questions, connects the disciplines, and helps build something better than what we just lost.
